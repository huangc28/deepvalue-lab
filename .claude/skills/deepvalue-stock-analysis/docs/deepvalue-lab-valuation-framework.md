# Reusable Stock Valuation and News Analysis Framework

Date: 2026-03-15

Purpose: a reusable framework for analyzing stocks where the logic, assumptions, and narrative matter more than any single-point valuation output.

## 1. Core principle

A good stock analysis is not just a number. It is a structured answer to three questions:

What kind of business is this?
What is the market already pricing in?
What future developments could cause the market's expectations to rise or fall?

Valuation is the output. The more important thing is the chain of reasoning.

## 2. Standard workflow

Step 1: Identify the business type
Before using any metric, classify the company.
Typical categories:
  Mature cash generator
  High-growth platform or compounder
  Financial institution
  Cyclical / commodity / semiconductor / capital-intensive business
  Turnaround / distressed business

Why this matters:
Different business types require different valuation anchors. Using the wrong metric creates false precision.

Step 2: Understand the economic engine
Ask:
  How does the company actually make money?
  What are the main revenue drivers?
  What determines margins?
  Is demand recurring, cyclical, regulated, or discretionary?
  Is growth driven by volume, price, mix, market share gains, or a new category?

Without this step, the valuation model is just arithmetic detached from business reality.

Step 3: Choose the right metrics
Examples:
  Mature companies: P/E, FCF yield, dividend yield
  Financials: P/B, ROE, credit quality, net interest margin
  Cyclicals: normalized EPS, EV/EBIT, mid-cycle margins, replacement economics
  Growth companies: revenue growth, gross margin, operating leverage, FCF potential, forward EPS, scenario multiples

The point is not to use every metric. The point is to use the metrics that best map to how value is created in that industry.

Step 4: Separate present facts from future expectations
Split the analysis into two layers.

Layer A: current facts
  trailing revenue, earnings, free cash flow
  balance sheet strength
  latest management guidance
  current valuation multiples

Layer B: future expectations
  what growth rate is implied by the current price?
  what margin assumptions is the market implicitly making?
  what would need to happen for the stock to deserve a higher or lower multiple?

Many mistakes happen because investors focus on current facts but ignore what is already embedded in the price.

Step 5: Build bear / base / bull scenarios
A scenario framework is usually better than a single target price.
For each scenario define:
  revenue growth path
  margin path
  capital intensity or FCF conversion if relevant
  terminal or target multiple
  key assumptions that would make the scenario true

The goal is not prediction. The goal is to see what assumptions are carrying the valuation.

Step 6: Identify variant perception
Ask:
  What does the market consensus seem to believe?
  Where might the consensus be too optimistic?
  Where might the consensus be too pessimistic?

This is often where returns come from. A stock is attractive when your reasoned view differs from consensus in the right direction and the market price does not fully reflect it.

## 3. How to integrate news into valuation work

News should not be treated as random headlines. It should be translated into valuation variables.

News categories to track:
  Earnings and guidance
  Product launches and technology transitions
  Regulation and legal developments
  Customer wins / losses and channel checks
  Capital allocation decisions
  Macro and industry cycle changes
  Competitive shifts

Convert news into model impact. Every important piece of news should be mapped to one or more of these:
  revenue growth changes
  gross margin changes
  operating expense changes
  capital expenditure changes
  valuation multiple changes
  probability shift between bear/base/bull scenarios

Examples:
  Strong earnings beat with raised guidance: may increase near-term revenue and improve confidence in the base or bull case.
  Export restriction: may lower revenue, compress margins, and reduce the multiple due to greater uncertainty.
  New large customer win: may improve revenue visibility, but could also raise concentration risk.
  Competitor breakthrough: may not affect this quarter's revenue, but may reduce terminal assumptions or future multiple.

This is the key mindset: news is only useful when it changes the model or the scenario probabilities.

## 4. Recommended analysis structure for any stock

Section A: Business and market context
  What does the company do?
  Why does the market care about it now?
  What are the main value drivers?

Section B: Current valuation snapshot
  market cap / enterprise value
  trailing and forward P/E if applicable
  EV/EBIT or EV/EBITDA if relevant
  price-to-sales if relevant
  free cash flow yield
  balance sheet summary

Section C: Quality check
  revenue quality
  margin profile
  ROIC / ROE if relevant
  balance sheet strength
  customer concentration
  cyclicality
  management credibility

Section D: News-driven interpretation
  For the recent quarter or recent news flow:
    what happened?
    why does it matter economically?
    which valuation inputs changed?
    which scenario became more or less likely?

Section E: Scenario valuation
  For each of bear, base, and bull:
    revenue assumption
    margin assumption
    EPS or FCF assumption
    target multiple
    fair value
    what must be true for this scenario to happen

Section F: Decision framing
  Do not end with "buy" or "don't buy" only. End with:
    what the current price implies
    what you think is most likely
    what would invalidate your thesis
    what signals you need to monitor next

## 5. Practical reasoning checklist

When analyzing a stock, ask these in order:
  What type of business is this?
  Which metrics fit this business?
  What is the market currently paying for it?
  What assumptions are embedded in that price?
  Which recent news items actually change those assumptions?
  How do those news items shift the bear/base/bull probabilities?
  Where is the main source of upside or downside?
  What evidence would prove me wrong?

## 6. Common mistakes to avoid

  Treating low multiples as automatic bargains
  Using the same metric for every industry
  Confusing a strong company with a cheap stock
  Focusing on headlines without translating them into valuation inputs
  Building one-point target prices without scenario ranges
  Ignoring balance sheet risk
  Ignoring customer concentration or regulatory risk
  Letting narrative outrun the numbers

## 7. Output template

Use this template when analyzing a stock:
  Business type and economic engine
  Current valuation snapshot
  Quality and risk review
  Recent news and what each item changes in the model
  Bear case
  Base case
  Bull case
  What the current price implies
  My provisional conclusion
  What I need to monitor next

## 8. Meta-principle

The goal is not to be impressed by a valuation model. The goal is to make the reasoning auditable.

A good analysis should make it clear:
  which assumptions matter most
  why the valuation changes when the news changes
  what you believe that the market may be missing
