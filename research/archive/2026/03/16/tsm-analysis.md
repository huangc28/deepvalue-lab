# TSM Analysis

Date: 2026-03-16  
Company: Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company  
Ticker: NYSE: TSM  
Point-in-time price: US$338.31 per ADR at the latest available NYSE close on 2026-03-13

## 1. Business Classification

TSMC is best classified as a high-quality, capital-intensive semiconductor infrastructure business with cyclical end-demand exposure, not a commodity foundry. That distinction matters because the right valuation anchor is forward earnings power with a capital-intensity and geopolitical haircut, not a trough-cycle commodity multiple.

The key reason is business mix and positioning. TSMC remains the dedicated IC foundry leader, and advanced nodes below 7nm contributed 74% of 2025 wafer revenue, with HPC already 58% of 2025 revenue. That makes earnings durability and technology leadership more decision-useful than a pure cycle call.

## 2. Thesis

The investable thesis is not simply "AI is strong." It is that TSMC has become the bottleneck owner of leading-edge compute and advanced packaging, so it can compound earnings faster and longer than a typical foundry, even while it absorbs a very large 2026-2027 capex wave.

At the current price, the bet is narrower than a classic deep-value idea. An investor is underwriting that AI and HPC demand remain strong enough to offset overseas fab dilution, N2 ramp inefficiency, and tariff-related weakness in consumer-oriented end markets. If that balance holds, the stock is still reasonable. If margin dilution bites harder than the market expects, downside opens quickly because the stock is no longer cheap on old foundry metrics.

## 3. Variant Perception

My read is that the market is no longer missing TSMC's quality, but it may still be underestimating how infrastructure-like the earnings base has become and overestimating how much near-term multiple expansion is left.

The bullish consensus is right about structural AI demand, but it can become too casual about two things: first, the real economic drag from overseas fab ramp and N2 start-up costs; second, the fact that a 30x neighborhood P/E already demands sustained high-20s growth or very high confidence in long-duration AI monetization. The more interesting variant is not "TSMC is dramatically mispriced." It is that TSMC is a better business than old foundry framing suggests, but also less obviously cheap than AI enthusiasm suggests.

## 4. Valuation Lens

Primary lens: forward ADR P/E.  
Secondary cross-check: trailing FCF yield, used cautiously.

Forward P/E fits best because TSMC is profitable, net-cash, and structurally differentiated. EV/EBITDA is less helpful here because it can flatter a business that is spending heavily just to preserve and extend its moat. A spot FCF yield is directionally useful, but 2026 capex is unusually elevated, so it is too distorted to be the main valuation anchor.

## 6. Recent News And News-To-Model Translation

1. 4Q25 results and 1Q26 guidance were stronger than a normal seasonal frame.
   4Q25 revenue reached US$33.73 billion, gross margin 62.3%, and 1Q26 guidance called for US$34.6 billion to US$35.8 billion revenue with 63% to 65% gross margin.
   News-to-model: this raises confidence that 2026 begins well above a normal seasonal pattern, so base-case revenue assumptions can reasonably sit near management's full-year target instead of sliding back toward low-20s growth.

2. Management explicitly forecast 2026 revenue to grow by close to 30% in US dollars, and said AI accelerators were already a high-teens percent share of 2025 revenue.
   News-to-model: this confirms AI is already material rather than optional upside. It pushes probability toward base and bull, and it supports a premium multiple versus old foundry history.

3. Management also warned that overseas fab ramp will dilute gross margin by roughly 2% to 3% in early stages and 3% to 4% later, while N2 ramp should dilute 2026 gross margin by another 2% to 3%.
   News-to-model: this is the main reason not to extrapolate current peak profitability too aggressively. It caps my base-case margin assumptions and keeps the bear case very live even if revenue growth remains healthy.

4. Management flagged tariff-policy and rising component-price risk, especially in consumer-related and price-sensitive segments.
   News-to-model: this does not yet break the thesis, but it lowers confidence in broad-based demand outside AI and keeps the valuation multiple from fully re-rating into "AI pure play" territory.

5. February 2026 monthly revenue was NT$317.66 billion, and January-February revenue was up 29.9% year over year.
   News-to-model: this is an important real-time confirmation that the near-30% 2026 growth framework is not just conference-call language. It supports the base case more than the bear case.

6. On February 10, 2026, the board approved about US$44.96 billion of capital appropriations and a capital injection of up to US$30 billion into TSMC Global.
   News-to-model: this reinforces that management is investing for multi-year demand, especially advanced process and packaging, but it also confirms rising capital intensity and makes a pure FCF-based bull argument less useful in the next 12 months.

## 7. Bear Case

- Operating assumptions: 2026 revenue of about US$149.4 billion, up about 22% year over year; net margin about 42.5%
- Valuation assumptions: ADR EPS about US$12.24; target P/E 24x
- Fair value: about US$294 per ADR
- What must be true: AI remains healthy but consumer weakness, tariff friction, or worse-than-expected overseas/N2 dilution prevents margin leverage from matching revenue growth

This is the scenario where TSMC still executes well operationally by historical standards, but the stock de-rates because growth is good rather than exceptional and margin quality softens.

## 8. Base Case

- Operating assumptions: 2026 revenue of about US$157.9 billion, up about 29% year over year; net margin about 44.0%
- Valuation assumptions: ADR EPS about US$13.40; target P/E 28x
- Fair value: about US$375 per ADR
- What must be true: management's near-30% revenue outlook broadly holds, advanced-node and CoWoS demand stays tight, and margin dilution is real but manageable rather than thesis-changing

This is the most defensible case today. It assumes strong growth persists, but it does not assume the market should pay an even more aggressive premium than it already has in prior enthusiasm periods.

## 9. Bull Case

- Operating assumptions: 2026 revenue of about US$164.0 billion, up about 34% year over year; net margin about 45.5%
- Valuation assumptions: ADR EPS about US$14.39; target P/E 31x
- Fair value: about US$446 per ADR
- What must be true: AI demand stays supply-constrained in a healthy way, advanced packaging remains a bottleneck in TSMC's favor, pricing discipline holds, and gross-margin dilution proves milder than current guidance language implies

This is possible, but it requires both revenue and execution to stay very strong while investors remain comfortable paying a premium multiple despite capex intensity and geopolitical overhang.

## 10. What The Current Price Implies

At US$338.31, the stock is not pricing in failure. If investors ultimately hold TSMC at about 26x forward earnings, the current price implies roughly US$13.0 of ADR EPS, which maps to something like US$153 billion of revenue at a 44% net margin, or roughly 25% growth versus 2025. That is already a strong outcome.

Put differently, the current price seems to underwrite a result between my bear and base cases, but much closer to base than to stress. The market is already giving TSMC credit for sustained AI demand and above-industry growth. What it is not fully paying for is a clean bull path with contained dilution. That is why the stock looks reasonable rather than obviously mispriced.

## 11. Provisional Conclusion

TSM looks fair to slightly attractive, not cheap.

The business quality is elite and the 2026 demand backdrop is strong enough to justify a premium valuation. But the current price already assumes continued high growth and does not leave a large margin of safety against the two variables most likely to cap returns in the next year: margin dilution from global expansion and N2 ramp, and any softening in non-AI demand due to tariffs or higher component costs.

If I had to reduce it to one underwriting judgment: the hidden support is that TSMC is more infrastructure-like than the old foundry label implies; the key risk is that investors treat that truth as a license to ignore capital intensity and dilution. That leads me to "reasonable if base case holds," not "deeply undervalued."

## 12. Thesis Status

Intact.

The core thesis that TSMC owns the most important manufacturing bottlenecks in advanced compute is reinforced, not weakened, by recent data. Revenue momentum, AI mix, and customer capacity signals remain supportive. I am not moving to `watch` because the business evidence is still strong. The reason I am not more aggressive is valuation discipline, not a broken business story.

## 14. What To Monitor Next

1. Whether 1Q26 revenue and gross margin land near the high end of guidance, because that will show whether the year is starting above, at, or below the current market's embedded expectations.
2. Whether monthly revenue keeps tracking close to the 30% full-year growth frame after January-February, especially if smartphone or consumer demand stays soft.
3. Whether overseas fab ramp and N2 dilution prove closer to the lower end or upper end of management's warning bands.
4. Whether advanced packaging capacity stays tight in a healthy way or begins to loosen, because that would change both growth confidence and multiple support.
5. Whether tariff or component-cost pressure begins to alter customer ordering behavior outside the AI buildout.
